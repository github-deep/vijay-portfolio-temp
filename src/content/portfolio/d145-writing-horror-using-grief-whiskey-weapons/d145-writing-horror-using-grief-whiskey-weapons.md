One of the most anticipated films in 2025 has been Weapons. Upon its release, the box office revenues stood at USD 260 million against a budget of USD 38 million and also garnered rave reviews from critics alike.

Written and Directed by Zach Cregger, Weapons made headlines when its script led to bidding wars among major studios. The script was sold within an hour of uploading!

So, what's so unique about this horror-mystery film?

Three things: Structure, Structure, and Structure.

Let's start with the logline of the film –

"When all but one child from the same class mysteriously vanish on the same night and at exactly the same time, a community is left questioning who or what is behind their disappearance."

The logline itself generates an intrigue to know what happened to those kids, where did they vanish, why was the lone boy left out, who is behind this, and why.

This setup is so much more compelling because it doesn't make sense. And our minds are determined to make sense of things that don't make sense. Now as an audience, we all are in to find out the truth.

The movie poster plays to the intrigue. Just these kids running outside in the middle of the night with their hands out is both creepy and original.

However, there are a plethora of other films that have been made with similar intrigue, so what makes Weapons stand out?

It's The Screenplay Structure.

Unlike other films, the film unfolds in chapters. The writer sets up the hook, and then reveals new information with each character (six in total). That is a refreshingly new take on horror-mystery. The structure jumps from one character to another as chapters in a book, with each character experiencing the same event from his or her point of view. With each chapter revealing new information, it builds up the crescendo to the finale, where all the chapters neatly tie into one chilling resolution.

The writer Cregger himself has revealed the inspiration for this structure – Magnolia (1999), another one of my favorite films, by the legendary Paul Thomas Anderson. In Magnolia too the narrative shifts from one character to another. Tracing further back, even Stanley Kubrick did something like this in parts of The Killing (1956), where the narrator strings together a robbery while moving between various characters whose portions are shown individually. However, unlike Magnolia, the genius of Weapons lies in episodic narrative style. In his own words, the writer explains – "It's a weird episodic thing that kind of jumps perspectives a lot. I was thinking what a fun way to orbit a central mystery, but tell it in this segmented way and let everyone kind of get closer and closer to solving it."

## Writing From Emotion, Not Ambition

Apart from the screenplay structure, the one quality that made this film unique is the process behind writing the film. But unlike screenplay structure, this quality is intangible.

Instead of figuring out the broad story first and then proceeding to write the scenes, the Writer-Director did the opposite; he wrote the script scene by scene without a broad story. That means he wrote the premise first, and then each day went further down digging for answers, just as we audience did watching the film.

"My best friend died in an accident that was really hard to understand. [Writing] was just like an emotional reaction to that. I was spared, because of my emotional pain, of writing from a place of ambition. I was writing from a place of catharsis. Writing where the process is the reward. Not to write a movie, not to write my next project, but to write because I needed to get this venom out. I started typing; I had no idea what the story was going to be. I literally went line by line. This is a true story. What is it? This teacher came to school and none of the kids were there. Okay, why? Yeah, they all ran away the night before. Okay, where'd they go? Nobody knows. Stephen King has that amazing metaphor where he's like, 'You need to be a paleontologist, and you're unearthing the dinosaur one bone at a time, but you don't know what the dinosaur is.' That's a beautiful way to create for me. Remove results from the process and just be discovery."

## Writing From The Personal Past

The film touches upon a few themes of grief, trauma, and power, and how these can be treated as weapons.

One of the strong themes in the film is alcoholism, a direct reference to the writer's personal life.

"The final chapter of this movie with Alex and the parents, that's autobiographical. I'm an alcoholic. I've been sober 10 years; my father died of cirrhosis. Living in a house with an alcoholic parent, the inversion of the family dynamic that happens. The idea that this foreign entity comes into your home, and it changes your parents, and you have to deal with this new behavioral pattern that you don't understand and don't have the equipment to deal with. But I don't care if any of this stuff comes through, the alcoholic metaphor is not important to me. I hope people have fun, honestly."

In one of the interesting scenes, the protagonist sees a giant gun in the sky. It directly points to the grief caused by gun violence in American schools, where kids die in school never to return home, just as is in the film. The title too is suggestive to this theme. Even though the writer has denied that this was his intention, connecting Weapons to school shootings is an obvious interpretation of the story.

The Master of Suspense, Alfred Hitchcock, once famously said, 'There is no terror in the bang, only in the anticipation of it'. Writer Zach Cregger followed it religiously and refreshingly!

Weapons stands beside modern horror landmarks such as Get Out, pushing the genre toward emotional realism rather than supernatural fear. Thank you, Cregger for writing this film, and thank you Weapons team, for bringing the script to life.
